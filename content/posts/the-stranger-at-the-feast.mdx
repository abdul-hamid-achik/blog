## The Woman in Gold

Let me tell you about Adele. She was born Adele Bauer in 1881, in Vienna, into a wealthy Jewish banking family. She married Ferdinand Bloch, who added her surname to his own, becoming Bloch-Bauer. She was intelligent, cultured, and by all accounts, restless â€” she hosted a salon that attracted some of Vienna's most prominent intellectuals. She was, in the language of her time, perfectly assimilated. She dressed right, spoke right, moved in the right circles. She was Viennese.

And yet. She was Jewish in a city that would, within three decades of her death, enthusiastically participate in the annihilation of its Jewish population. She belonged and she didn't belong. She was inside the room and outside the room at the same time.

Klimt painted her twice. The first portrait â€” the one we're looking at â€” was completed in 1907. It took three years. Klimt made over one hundred preparatory sketches. And what he produced was not so much a portrait as a gilded cage. The gold leaf technique Klimt used was borrowed from Byzantine iconography â€” the same technique used to paint saints and madonnas. Klimt turned Adele into an icon. He made her sacred. But in doing so, he made her less human. The gold covers her body, merges with her dress, absorbs the chair she sits in until there is no boundary between the woman and the decoration. Where does Adele end and the ornament begin?

This is the visual equivalent of what Georg Simmel, the German sociologist, described in his 1908 essay "The Stranger" â€” a concept I didn't learn about in school, because I didn't study sociology, but that I stumbled upon one night while reading something else entirely, and it hit me the way certain things hit you when you've been carrying them without knowing their name.

## Simmel's Stranger

Simmel's stranger is not the outsider who has never been part of the group. That would be too simple. The stranger, Simmel says, is someone who comes today and stays tomorrow â€” who is part of the group but not entirely of it. The stranger is both near and far, present and absent, inside and outside. They share the space with everyone else but they are not rooted in it the way the others are. They arrived from somewhere else, and that somewhere else clings to them like a smell, like an accent, like a name.

## Sartre's Look

Jean-Paul Sartre, in "Being and Nothingness," described what he called le regard â€” the Look. The moment when another person's gaze falls on you and you become, in that instant, an object. Before the Look, you exist for yourself â€” you are a subject, a consciousness, a freedom. After the Look, you become a thing that is seen. You become what the other person sees.

## Lacan's Gaze

Lacan took Sartre's Look and went deeper. For Lacan, the Gaze is not just the moment of being seen by another person. The Gaze is structural. It is the feeling of being watched by something you can't see â€” the sense that the world itself is looking at you, that there is an eye somewhere that you can never locate but that is always, always there.

This is the Gaze that Klimt painted. Look at Adele again. Her eyes are not looking at you. They are looking slightly to the side, slightly down. But you feel watched. The painting watches you. The gold watches you. The ornamental spirals and triangles are like a thousand eyes, decorative but relentless. This is what Lacan means by the scopic drive â€” the drive to see and to be seen, and the anxiety that comes with knowing that you are always already being looked at, that you can never escape the Gaze, that there is no position from which you are not observed.

For the stranger, this Gaze is intensified. The insider can forget they are being watched because the Gaze confirms their belonging â€” they are seen as they expect to be seen. The stranger can never forget, because the Gaze always reveals the gap between who they are and who they are perceived to be. The stranger lives in that gap. The stranger is that gap.

I live in that gap. ## The Golden Cage

Let me return to Klimt. Let me return to the gold.

There is a reading of this painting that sees the gold as celebration â€” Klimt honoring Adele, crowning her in the visual language of sacred art, elevating her to the status of a Byzantine Madonna. Adele belonged to Viennese society. She was accepted, admired, celebrated. But the price of that acceptance was this: she had to become gold. She had to become ornament. She had to allow herself to be absorbed into the decorative surface, to let the patterns and spirals and geometric shapes consume her individual features until she was less a person and more a beautiful thing. You learn to pronounce your own name the way others find comfortable â€” "Just call me Abdo, it's easier" â€” and something inside you folds a little bit every time you do it. You become gold. ## The Stranger's Gift

But here is the thing that Simmel understood, and that Sartre and Lacan, for all their brilliance, sometimes forgot: the stranger's position is not only suffering. It is also sight.

The stranger sees what the native cannot see. Because the stranger has nothing invested in the group's illusions, the stranger can perceive the structures that the insiders take for granted. The stranger can say: the emperor has no clothes. The stranger can look at the golden surface and see the cage underneath. The stranger can sit at the feast and notice that the music is covering up the silence.
