Giorgio de Chirico painted "The Soothsayer's Recompense" in 1913, during the period when he was inventing what he called *pittura metafisica* — metaphysical painting. The title alone is an enigma: what is the soothsayer's recompense? What does the one who sees the future receive in return for that vision? De Chirico never tells us. He only shows us the landscape that the answer inhabits.

The scene is one of de Chirico's characteristic deserted piazzas — a space emptied of human activity but filled with the residue of human presence. Dark arcades recede into shadow. A clock tower marks time that seems to have stopped. A train departs in the distance, heading somewhere we cannot follow. And in the center of this emptied world lies the sleeping figure of Ariadne — the Cretan princess of Greek myth who gave Theseus the thread to navigate the Minotaur's labyrinth, only to be abandoned by him on the island of Naxos.

Ariadne sleeps. She is the soothsayer who provided the key to the labyrinth but could not foresee her own abandonment. Her recompense is this: to sleep forever in a piazza where time has stopped, where the train leaves without her, where the shadows fall at angles that no real sun could produce.

De Chirico's metaphysical paintings were a direct precursor to Surrealism. André Breton called them revelations. They showed that painting could access the same territory that Freud was mapping in his psychoanalytic work — the territory of the unconscious, where logic dissolves, where objects carry emotional weight that exceeds their physical form, where the familiar becomes uncanny.

The painting now resides in the Philadelphia Museum of Art, far from the Italian piazzas it depicts, still asking its unanswerable question: what does it cost to see what others cannot?
