Odilon Redon's "The Cyclops" is a painting that should be terrifying and is instead unbearably tender. The one-eyed giant Polyphemus peers over a ridge at the sleeping nymph Galatea below, and in his single enormous eye there is not menace but something closer to worship. He is the monster who loves what he cannot touch. He is the seer whose vision is both his gift and his curse.

Redon was the great painter of the invisible. While the Impressionists captured light as it fell on water lilies and cathedral facades, Redon painted what happened behind the eyelids when you closed your eyes. He painted dreams, visions, hallucinations, the creatures that live in the spaces between waking and sleeping. He once wrote: "My originality consists in bringing to life, in a human way, improbable beings and making them live according to the laws of probability, by putting — as far as possible — the logic of the visible at the service of the invisible."

The single eye of the Cyclops is the most powerful symbol in the painting. In mythology, Polyphemus is a monster — the cave-dwelling cannibal who traps Odysseus and eats his men. But Redon strips away the horror and finds something else: the archetype of the seer, the one who sees with a single, focused gaze that penetrates beyond the surface of things. The Cyclops' eye is the third eye of mystical tradition — the eye of intuition, of clairvoyance, of the vision that sees not what is but what lies beneath.

This is the eye of the tarot reader, the analyst, the artist. The eye that looks at a spread of cards or a patient's dream or a blank canvas and sees the patterns that others miss. Redon understood that this kind of vision comes at a cost. The Cyclops sees deeply, but he sees alone. His vision separates him from the world it reveals.

The painting hangs in the Kröller-Müller Museum in Otterlo, Netherlands, where its luminous colors — impossible greens and golds and blues — glow with a light that seems to come from inside the painting rather than falling on it from outside.
