Edvard Munch's *Jealousy* does not really paint a triangle. It paints a position: the place from which the jealous subject watches a scene that seems to continue without him. That is why the man in the foreground is so unbearable. He is closest to us, almost pressed against the surface of the canvas, and yet he is the one furthest from contact.

The painting divides space cruelly. On the right, the man's face rises out of a dark mass of black clothing, pale green and violet, with a red beard and exhausted eyes. He does not look at us with dignity. He looks like someone who has already spent too much time imagining what is happening behind him. In the background, beneath trees dotted with red fruit, a man in shadow stands beside a woman whose red dress opens around a pale body. They are less individualized than the sufferer in front. Jealousy is like that: the rival becomes a silhouette, the beloved becomes a scene, and all precision is reserved for the wound.

At the far left, a strip of ochre wall and a reddish plant hold another temperature entirely, like a domestic world from which the foreground figure has already been exiled. That detail matters. Jealousy is often mistaken for an excess of love. Munch suggests something colder. It is an optical disorder produced by humiliation. The jealous person does not simply want the other; he becomes trapped in endless spectatorship. He cannot enter the scene, cannot leave it, and cannot stop replaying it.

That is why the face in front looks already half-emptied out, as if the act of looking had consumed more of him than the rival ever could. When Dostoevsky's characters become jealous, something similar happens: desire curdles into self-surveillance. The jealous subject begins to inhabit the place of his own exclusion. That is why this painting belongs near <Link href="/posts/the-gambler-and-the-black-square">The Gambler and the Black Square</Link>. Not because the story is the same, but because the psychic mechanism is. Obsession turns the world into a theater built to injure you.

Munch leaves the background couple almost dreamlike. They are not the truth of the picture. The truth is the face in front, the one that cannot stop seeing. *Jealousy* is not passion at its hottest here. It is passion after it has curdled into repetition.

The punishment is not betrayal. The punishment is remaining in the room long after the scene has already happened.
