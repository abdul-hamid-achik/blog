Jean Delville's "The Treasures of Satan" is a painting that looks like it was dreamed by someone who had read too much occult philosophy and not enough — which is to say, it looks exactly like what it is: the work of a true believer. Delville was not merely a painter who used esoteric imagery for aesthetic effect. He was a Theosophist, a member of Joséphin Péladan's Rosicrucian order, the Salon de la Rose+Croix, a man who believed that art was a form of spiritual practice and that the painter's task was to make the invisible hierarchies of the cosmos visible on canvas.

In this monumental work, Satan hovers above an oceanic abyss filled with intertwined human bodies — souls trapped in sensual pleasure, tangled together in a mass of flesh and pearls and coral. They are not suffering in the conventional sense. They are slumbering. They are dreaming a dream of material satisfaction that they mistake for reality, and the treasures that surround them — gold, jewels, the pleasures of the body — are the chains that keep them submerged.

The connection to tarot is not accidental. Delville moved in the same esoteric circles that would, fourteen years later, produce the Rider-Waite-Smith tarot deck — the most influential tarot deck in history. The Devil card, number XV of the Major Arcana, depicts precisely what Delville painted: figures chained to material illusion, bound not by force but by attachment, by the inability to see that the chains are loose enough to remove.

Carl Jung would later call this the Shadow — the part of the psyche that we refuse to acknowledge, the desires and impulses that we push into the unconscious because they threaten our self-image. The Treasures of Satan is a portrait of the Shadow made magnificent. Delville does not condemn the souls in the abyss. He paints them with the same beauty he gives to Satan himself. The temptation is real because it is beautiful. The illusion works because it feels like truth.

The painting hangs in the Royal Museums of Fine Arts of Belgium in Brussels, where it continues to do what Delville intended: to show you the beauty of what binds you, and to ask whether knowing the chains are there is enough to make you want to take them off.
